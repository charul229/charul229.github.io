Moving contact line survey
Hydrodynamc model of steady movement of solid-liquid-fluid system contact line_JCIS_1971_Huh & Scriven

This is the first paper which explains the dynamics of moving contact line in two phases. The model aims to clarify that no-slip boundary condition causes non-integrable singularity at the contact line. Their study reports two parameters namely viscosity ratio and dynamic contact angle to be flow characterizing parameters. The key inference from the theory was that more viscous fluid largely shows a rolling motion near the moving contact line.

On the motion of fluid-fluid interface along the solid surface_Dussan_1974_JFM

Dussan and Davis, 1974,JFM, examined the kinemtaics of moving contact lines in general and showed that contact line motion is impossible if no-slip condition holds and showed that drop rolls along the solid.

The physics of moving wetting line_Blake_2006_Blake

This paper explains the theoretical models developed till that data do not precisely predict the dynamics occurring near the moving contact line. There is a second approach to spreading which involves zero bulk flow of the liquid but instead postulates that molecules from the liquid near the contact-line jump across and attach to the substrate effectively moving the contact line forward; this gives the molecular kinetic theory (MKT). HDT and MKT... HDT works for free spreading of drop as there are only natural time scales are invloved in the problem but in forced spreading case there is one more time scale involves which is related to relaxation which causes the dynamics to be different from free spreading case. For forced spreading case, MKT works well.

Forced wetting and Hydrodynamic assist_PoF_Blake2015

This paper explains the mechanism of hydrodynamic assist which helps to prevent the air entrainment in advancing contact line motion. It delays the wetting failure by increasing the critical speed at which failure occurs. It is simply the degree to which the flow influences the dynamic contact angle that increases during forced wetting.
